I want to be upfront: this isn't a hit piece on Substack. The platform worked well for me for two years. I grew from zero to about 800 subscribers, the reading experience was good, and the payments infrastructure was painless.

I left because I realized I was building on rented land.

## The trigger

It wasn't one thing. It was an accumulation:

- Substack's recommendation algorithm started surfacing my posts in ways I couldn't control or predict
- The "Notes" feature turned my publication into a social media profile I didn't ask for
- Export gave me a CSV of email addresses and a folder of markdown, but no redirects — every link to my old posts would eventually break or point to a page asking readers to subscribe to other newsletters

That last one is what did it. My archive isn't Substack's marketing surface. It's my work.

## The move

I set up a site on my own domain with IndiePub. Migrated the archive (every post, every URL). Set up redirects from the old Substack URLs. Moved my subscriber list over.

The whole process took a weekend. The hardest part was writing the "I've moved" email and resisting the urge to over-explain.

## What I gained

- **My URLs** — they'll work as long as I pay for the domain
- **Full control over the reading experience** — no algorithmic sidebar, no "subscribe to similar writers" prompts on my pages
- **RSS** — readers can follow me in their feed reader without giving me their email
- **Ownership of the relationship** — my subscriber list isn't locked in a platform that might change its terms

## What I lost

Discoverability. Substack's network effect is real. New subscribers don't find me through an algorithm anymore. They find me because someone shared a link, or because they searched for something I wrote.

That's a trade I'm willing to make.
