Last month, the *Oakdale Register* shut down after 114 years. The story made national news for about six hours, which is roughly six hours more than most local paper closures get.

But here's what the obituaries miss: the *Register* wasn't killed by the internet. It was killed by a private equity firm that bought it in 2021, stripped the staff to four people, and expected them to fill a daily paper while also "pivoting to digital."

## The coverage gap

When a local paper dies, the things it covered don't stop happening. City council still meets every Tuesday. The school board still votes on budgets. The water authority still files reports.

What changes is that nobody's watching.

I spent three weeks attending city council meetings in towns that lost their paper in the last two years. Here's what I found:

- **Public comment periods shortened** from 30 minutes to 10 in two of three cities
- **FOIA response times increased** — without reporters filing regular requests, response windows stretched from days to months
- **Attendance dropped** at public meetings. When nobody's writing about it, fewer people show up

## What's replacing papers

In two of the three cities I visited, the gap was filled by a mix of Facebook groups, a Nextdoor feed, and a part-time "community news" site run by someone who also sells real estate.

That's not journalism. It's not even close.

## What could work

The models that are actually working share a few traits:

1. **Nonprofit structure** — removes the growth-at-all-costs pressure
2. **Narrow focus** — cover the school board *or* city hall, not everything
3. **Reader funding** — subscriptions, not ads
4. **One or two people** — lean enough to survive on community support

The future of local news might not look like a newspaper at all. It might look like a newsletter someone writes from their kitchen table, published on a site they own, funded by readers who care about their city.

That's not a downgrade. It might be an upgrade.
